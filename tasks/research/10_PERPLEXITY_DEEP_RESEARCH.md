# Enterprise Evaluation and Procurement of AI Agents (2025-2026)

Source: Perplexity Deep Research, April 2026

---

## Executive Summary

Enterprise procurement of AI agents in 2025-2026 is characterized by larger, more technical buying committees, mandatory proofs of concept (POCs), and heightened scrutiny of business value and risk. Benchmark data suggests that enterprise AI procurement cycles now average roughly **seven months** end to end, with security and compliance alone often adding six weeks. Most mid-to-large enterprises insist on a POC or pilot, typically budgeted in the **low five figures** and run over two to eight weeks, yet analyst firms report that **30% or more** of generative-AI projects are abandoned after POC due to weak data, unclear value, or governance gaps.

Gartner, Forrester, and RAND all highlight a growing mismatch between AI's pace of change and traditional acquisition processes, leading CIOs to question legacy RFP-centric vendor selection and to experiment with trial- and outcome-based evaluation models. Surveys from Dataiku and Gartner-referenced CIO commentary show that **a majority of CIOs now regret at least one major AI vendor/platform decision**, citing AI-washing, vendor lock-in, lack of traceability, and pilots that work in isolation but fail to scale.

## How Enterprises Evaluate AI Agents

### Buying Committees and Stakeholders

Enterprise AI buying committees now include an average of **more than eleven stakeholders**, significantly larger than for traditional software purchases. Forrester's "State of Business Buying, 2026" notes that B2B buying has become more collaborative and risk-sensitive, with larger groups, earlier procurement involvement, and a growing reliance on trials for AI-infused offerings. In practice, AI agent evaluations typically involve IT, security, data/ML leadership, line-of-business owners, procurement, legal, and risk/compliance teams.

### Evaluation Workflow

Across sources, a common enterprise evaluation pattern for AI agents emerges:

1. **Problem and use-case definition** - Clarify business outcome, constraints, and build-vs-buy options
2. **Market scan and vendor shortlisting** - AI-assisted research with peer references; shortlists average ~4 vendors
3. **Architecture and integration fit** - Deep technical workshops on data access, orchestration, observability
4. **Risk, security, and compliance review** - Data residency, privacy, model governance, auditability; adds ~6 weeks
5. **Proof of concept / trial** - Scoped POC on production-like data to validate accuracy, workflow fit
6. **Commercial negotiation and scaling** - Transition to production with phased rollout and SLAs

Forrester stresses that trials need to be structured as "strategic conversion tools" with clearly defined real-world use cases, guided onboarding, and success metrics, not just product previews.

## POC Economics for AI Agents

### Typical POC Cost Ranges

- POC/proof-of-concept band: **$2,000-$20,000**, with industry average of **$5,000-$20,000** over 2-8 weeks (Softermii, 2026)
- Enterprise-grade AI agent POCs (heavier integration + compliance): **$12,000-$20,000**
- Prototype builds: $2,000-$10,000; enterprise agents exceeding **$100,000+** for full production
- POCs are usually a small fraction of total implementation cost

### POC Duration and Scope

AI agent POCs generally run from **5 days to 4 weeks**, with an industry average of **2-8 weeks**. These POCs usually demonstrate feasibility on a narrow workflow using production data slice and limited users.

Warning: Under-funded POCs may be too shallow to surface reliability/governance issues. Over-engineered POCs risk becoming slow multi-vendor bake-offs. This is driving interest in "production probation" periods with clear success metrics.

## Procurement Cycle Length

### Quantitative Benchmarks

- AI procurement cycles average **7.2 months**, about **40% longer** than equivalent non-AI software purchases
- **72%** of enterprises require a POC before committing to AI vendor contracts
- Security and compliance reviews alone add **6 weeks** to AI procurement timelines
- RAND notes an "acquisition speed mismatch" - traditional procurement too slow for AI's development pace

### Variation by Sector

- Highly regulated industries (financial services, healthcare, defense): **12-18 month** timelines
- Organizations with AI-ready data: implement **3x faster** than peers with fragmented data
- Federal acquisition: emerging "software fast track" pathways attempting to compress cycles

## POC and Pilot Failure / Abandonment Rates

### Gartner Projections

- At least **30%** of generative-AI projects abandoned after POC by end of 2025
- Primary causes: poor data quality, inadequate risk controls, escalating costs, unclear business value
- In practice, abandonment rates may approach **50%** with "lack of business value" as key reason
- GenAI in procurement described as entering a "trough of disillusionment"

### Pilot Conversion to Production

- ~50% of procurement teams piloted generative AI in 2024
- Only **~4%** had achieved large-scale deployment by 2025
- Just over **one-third** of organizations report "meaningful" GenAI implementations
- Most AI pilots stall, remain in narrow use cases, or fail to demonstrate sufficient value

## Gartner, Forrester, and RAND Perspectives

### Gartner: Disillusionment, Data Gaps, and Abandonment

- ~59% of finance functions already using AI, but slowed by complexity, data challenges, talent shortages
- Core issue is not AI capability but maturity of enterprise data, governance, and evaluation frameworks
- GenAI positioned in "trough of disillusionment" in Hype Cycle for Procurement

### Forrester: Skeptical Buyers and Proof-First Procurement

- **19%** of business buyers less confident in decisions because AI produced inaccurate/misleading results
- Forrester predicts a **Fortune 500 company will sue a B2B provider by 2026** for AI-generated misrepresentation
- Procurement professionals involved in **~60%** of B2B deals
- AI-related purchases: more collaborative, risk-sensitive, dependent on validation from trusted sources

### RAND: Speed and Fragmentation

- Current acquisition efforts "fragmented and duplicative" with multiple organizations buying overlapping tools
- Traditional hardware/software acquisition models don't fit AI's rapid iteration and data dependencies
- Need for enterprise-level oversight, flexible acquisition strategies, standardized metrics

## CIO Sentiment and "Broken" AI Vendor Evaluation

### Regret and Vendor Lock-In

- **Nearly 3 in 4 CIOs (74%)** regret a significant AI vendor/platform decision made in prior 18 months (Dataiku survey, 600 CIOs, 2026)
- **62%** face direct scrutiny from CEOs about AI vendor choices
- **71%** of CIOs expect AI budgets could be reduced/frozen if targets not met by mid-2026
- **85%** say gaps in traceability and explainability have hindered or stalled AI projects

CIO practitioner commentary: "We bought into AI-washing, accepted closed ecosystems, and ignored the looming threat of vendor lock-in. The real leaders are now those prioritizing modular architectures and data sovereignty over cool demos."

### RFP Fatigue and Rise of POCs

- Classical RFP processes misaligned with AI's pace — "procurement gap" where RFP cycles outlast technology shifts
- Generative AI makes vendor responses look uniformly strong on paper
- Shift toward 2-4 week POC-driven evaluation on real data
- CIO feedback: "Use cases can be great in the pilot, but not good options to scale"
- Moving "beyond proof of concept" with measurable value remains central challenge

## Implications for Better AI-Agent Evaluation

Design principles emerging from the data:

1. **Separate AI procurement from generic software procurement** — AI agents bring new variables (model behavior, data governance, prompt security, observability) that standard RFPs don't capture
2. **Treat POCs as structured experiments** — Clear success metrics, production-like data, tied to go/no-go decisions
3. **Invest in data readiness before evaluation** — Non-AI-ready data is the primary barrier
4. **Prioritize modular, open architectures** — CIOs want agents that can be swapped or layered via APIs
5. **Align incentives and governance** — Evaluation must incorporate risk, legal, and compliance from the outset

**The organizations that progress fastest are those that standardize AI-specific evaluation criteria, shorten learning cycles through structured trials, and maintain flexibility rather than locking into early bets.**
