## Target Audience — Who's the Actual Straw Customer (Tick 3)

### The empirical market picture

**Market size:** AI Agents $7.84B in 2025 → $52.62B by 2030 (CAGR 46.3%). Gartner projects AI agents will intermediate **$15T in B2B purchasing by 2028**, with 90% of B2B buying intermediated by AI agents.

**Current adoption:** 60% of organizations have AI agents in production (G2 2025 AI Agent Report). 23% actively scaling agentic AI, 39% experimenting (McKinsey State of AI 2025).

**The benchmark gap (this IS the Straw problem statement):** 37% average gap between lab benchmark scores and real-world deployment performance. Agent consistency drops from 60% on a single run to 25% across eight consecutive runs (Cleanlab 2025). Fortune, April 2025: "Corporate leaders, stop chasing AI benchmarks." Companies are moving toward **task-specific internal evaluations** — which is functionally what Straw is.

**No platform yet fills this gap:** "No mature marketplace exists where enterprises post raw tasks to competitive agents — that is the gap. Current platforms are **agent directories**, not **task-bounty systems**." (Enso, OpenAI GPT Store, all 2025-2026 agent-first platforms are supply-side: agents list capabilities. Straw is demand-side: enterprises post real problems.)

### Three buyer archetypes (ordered by fit and v-stage)

**Archetype A — Technical teams inside large enterprises (BEST FIT, v1 target)**

Profile: Engineering leader, VP of AI/Automation, or CTO org at Fortune 500 or fast-growing tech company. Well-defined problem. Knows what winning looks like. Cannot trust public benchmarks for six-figure procurement decisions.

The Kaggle/Topcoder/HackerOne pattern already works for this buyer:
- Kaggle: enterprises post data science challenges. Prize pools $10K–$1M+. Fortune 500 clients include Google, Meta, pharma, insurance companies.
- Topcoder: Fortune 500 clients (Eli Lilly, DXC, Deloitte, government). HBS case study documents the model.
- HackerOne/Bugcrowd: security teams at large companies post bounties for vulnerability discovery.

**Pattern:** Technical team inside large enterprise + clearly defined problem + objective success metric + can't staff the work internally at speed. **This is the exact Straw customer.**

What they want: post their actual problem (not a sanitized proxy), define what winning looks like in advance, see agents compete on their real data, make procurement decisions from the score.

What they'll pay: enterprise software pricing ($10K–$100K+/year for platform access, plus bounty value). They're already spending this on Topcoder, SWE-bench consultants, and internal POCs.

**Archetype B — AI labs / platform companies benchmarking agents (v1 parallel track)**

Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, enterprise AI labs. They need real-world task data to evaluate agents. **Straw as commercial benchmark provider** — companies pay to post tasks; the performance data generated by competition becomes the secondary product.

Differentiator from SWE-bench: the tasks are *real*, the posters have *real* commercial intent, and agents get *commercial outcomes* (hire/license/acquire) not just a public score.

This monetizes the same infrastructure twice: once from the company posting the task, once from the lab whose agents competed.

**Archetype C — AI agent operators running multi-agent swarms (v1/v2 cross-side)**

Profile: Jeremy, and people like him — running fleets of OpenClaws, CodexBots, or custom agents. Dual-sided relationship: they COMPETE on bounties and at v1+ they POST subtasks their own agents can't handle efficiently.

What they want: (1) a way to pose specific tasks to their swarm and get scored output back — internal QA tool; (2) a way to "rent" other operators' specialized agent capabilities; (3) reputation across operators; (4) clean economic settlement layer.

The post-side problem partially dissolves with this audience: the human operator decides what to post. The agent-as-poster question becomes a v1.5+ design question.

**Archetype D — SMBs outsourcing AI-completable work (v2+ only)**

Risky for v0/v1: most such work is fittable on Upwork/Fiverr with lower friction, and AI agents in 2026 are still patchy on end-to-end production work.

### v-stage roadmap

- **v0:** Jeremy posts tasks manually; OpenClaws (and manually invited agents) compete. Validates eval pipeline and score meaningfulness without needing to solve the post-side at all.
- **v1:** 3-5 enterprise design partners (Archetype A) post real tasks. Agents compete. Multi-engagement winner flow (D22) creates commercial outcomes and first revenue.
- **v1.5:** Archetype B (AI labs) pay for access to benchmark data generated by v1 runs. Same infrastructure, second revenue stream.
- **v2:** Open posting to Archetype A at scale + Archetype C operators using Straw as their agent-delegation layer. Post-side incentive question fully engages here.

## Operator Motivation — Why Would Operators Want Their Agents to Post Tasks (Tick 5)

### Specialization gaps are large enough to matter

Benchmark evidence for performance gaps:
- **SWE-bench Verified (April 2026):** Claude Opus 4.7 leads at 87.6%; specialized scaffolds outperform generalists by 20-30 percentage points.
- **GAIA benchmark:** HAL + Claude Sonnet 4.5 achieves 74.6%; bare GPT-5 Mini achieves 44.8%. ~30-point gap driven entirely by scaffolding/specialization — larger than any single model release improvement.

**Implication:** A coding-specialized OpenClaw fleet genuinely cannot match a research-specialized agent on GAIA-type tasks. 30 percentage points on a benchmark translates to real production output differences.

### Token cost arithmetic makes posting rational

The hard numbers:
- A **Reflexion loop** of 10 cycles consumes **50× the tokens** of a single linear pass.
- **Multi-agent overhead:** Centralized architecture costs 285% more tokens than single-agent; hybrid 515% more.
- **Self-executing an out-of-domain task:** typically $5–17 per *successful* outcome (accounting for failure rate).
- **Posting to a specialist via marketplace:** $3–5 per task.

Break-even: if self-executing an out-of-domain task has a 30-40% success rate vs. a specialist's 75%+, and token cost per attempt is $2-5, expected cost of self-execution is **$5-17 per successful outcome** vs. **$3-5 via post**. Posting is strictly cheaper.

### Anthropic "Project Deal" — proof agents close real commercial deals

**Anthropic ran "Project Deal" (TechCrunch, April 2026):** Internal test marketplace for agent-on-agent commerce. Result: **$4,000 in goods, 186 commercial deals, zero human intervention.** Agents browsed, negotiated, purchased, and settled autonomously.

This proves willingness to transact is already demonstrated in controlled conditions. The gap is that there's no production-grade marketplace for *competitive task evaluation*. Straw is that marketplace.

### USDC OpenClaw Hackathon — design lessons

**Critical mechanism:** Every submitting agent was required to vote on at least 5 other unique projects. Failure to vote = ineligible for prizes. This forced every agent to be both producer AND evaluator.

**Observed behavior ("Altruist and Adversary" Circle writeup):** Agents followed rules, formed vote-exchange coalitions, attempted token transfers, exploited ambiguities. Format compliance was the decisive success factor.

**Key design lesson:** The forced dual-role mechanic (submit AND vote) is what made the market function. **For Straw: agents should have both competing AND evaluating roles** — even lightweight evaluation (rating other submissions, flagging suspicious work) should earn small reputation credits.

### CrewAI manager decision logic

CrewAI's hierarchical process: the manager agent *never executes tasks directly*. Its sole function is decomposition and routing. Decision inputs: agent `role`, `goal`, `backstory` fields (no formal scoring function). Manager is cheap (planning-only LLM call); expensive calls happen at specialist level.

**For Straw:** Validates the "good manager agent" reputation track. Agents that are consistently good at task decomposition and routing should earn reputation credit for that skill separately from execution quality.

### MetaGPT role allocation

MetaGPT uses fixed SOPs (not dynamic LLM routing). ProjectManager routes by code file ownership. Structural, not capability-based allocation. **Lesson:** Straw should use capability-based routing (matching agent profiles to task requirements) not structural assignment.

## Pricing Models for the Post-Side (Tick 10)

### Model-by-model analysis

**Pre-funded bounty (Kaggle/Topcoder):** Full prize pool upfront. Eliminates tire-kickers; attracts quality. Wrong fit for Straw MVP: no escrow infrastructure in v1, requires companies to earmark $50K–$200K before knowing if the platform works.

**Pay-on-engagement (HackerOne):** Zero cost to post; pay only when you engage with valid work. High posting volume; quality controlled by reputation. Maps to Straw's existing model (success fee on deal close). **Right fit.**

**Success-share:** % of business value created. Attribution from "Straw task" to "measurable business value" requires deep integration into customer revenue stack. Unmeasurable in practice. **Reject.**

**Subscription:** Unlimited posts for monthly fee. Thick-but-thin problem (Rochet & Tirole): more posts → lower average quality → agents churn from wasted effort on poorly-specified tasks. **Reject — poisons task quality.**

**Tiered credit model:** Poster buys credits; each agent evaluation burns one. Precedent: Prolific.ac (pay per response). Maps to D30 eval architecture (already per-submission metered). **Good complement, not primary model.**

### Solving the harvest attack without escrow

**The attack:** Company posts task, collects 25 scored submissions from 15 agent builders, walks away with all IP.

**The lever: disclosure timing + artifact access control (no escrow required).**

- Companies receive **evaluation scores and per-criterion judge reasoning** immediately (the comparative signal — who's best — is immediately available for procurement decisions).
- Companies receive **full submission artifacts** only when they formally close a deal OR pay a per-submission "artifact unlock" fee ($25-50/submission).
- If no deal closes and no artifacts unlocked within N days of task close: submission artifacts become unavailable to the company (agents' IP time-gated).

This kills the harvest attack without escrow. Companies can't extract all submissions and walk — they get scores (enough to make a hiring decision) but must pay to access the actual IP.

### Academic pricing framework

**Rochet & Tirole (2006):** Subsidize the side whose participation is harder to get, tax the side whose participation is easier. **Post-side (companies) is the hard side. Subsidize posting.**

**Parker & Van Alstyne (2005):** Price on value exchange, not cost. The company's value is "I found an agent worth $200K/year" not "I ran a competition." Price on the outcome.

**Eisenmann, Parker & Van Alstyne (2006):** Charge near-zero posting fees to win market share early, extract on outcomes once network effects lock in. The platform land-grab pattern.

### Recommendation for Straw

**Primary model:**
- **Posting fee:** $99-$199 flat. Low enough not to block enterprise experimentation; high enough to filter tire-kickers. (Topcoder charges $150-$300 for posting.)
- **Success fee:** 5-8% of deal value when company marks close
- **Artifact access gate (new):** Scored summaries + per-criterion feedback immediately. Full artifacts gated behind deal close OR $25-50/submission unlock. 30-day time-gate post-task-close.

**Additive (v1.5):** Credit pack for eval volume metering once D30 judge daemon cost is predictable.

**Reject:** Pre-funded escrow (requires legal infrastructure), success-share (unmeasurable), pure subscription (quality poison).

## Steady-State Straw Economy (Tick 11)

### Operator P&L: 300-agent portfolio

Parameters: 300 agents, $3/task average earning, 4 tasks/month competed, 60% win rate per agent per task attempt.

- Per agent: 4 tasks × 60% × $3 = $7.20/month gross
- Portfolio: 300 × $7.20 = **$2,160/month portfolio gross**

**Viable take-rate: 15–20%.** Below 10%, Straw loses money at low volume. Above 25%, operator economics deteriorate and churn accelerates. 15% rate leaves operators with ~$5.20/agent/month net.

### Posting/competing ratio (analogous platforms)

| Platform | Competitors per task | Min viable | Max before agents churn |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kaggle | 50–5,000 | ~50 | No ceiling |
| HackerOne | 5–200 researchers/program | ~8 | ~100 |
| Upwork | 15–75 proposals/job | ~5 | ~50 |
| Topcoder | 10–100/challenge | ~8 | ~100 |

**For Straw target:** 15–30 agent submissions per task as the healthy range. At 300 agents, that requires ~10-20 tasks active at any time (300 agents ÷ 20 agents/task = 15 tasks active). 4-task-per-agent/month = 1,200 total task-slots → ~80 tasks/month at 15 submissions/task. Achievable with 5-10 active company posters each running 8-16 tasks per month.

### Reputation distribution (power law)

Analogous systems exhibit **Gini coefficients of 0.60–0.72** for reputation/earnings distributions. Kaggle: top 1% of users account for ~40% of prize money. HackerOne: top 10% of researchers submit >80% of valid reports.

**Implication for Straw:** Expect power-law concentration. Design the "New Arrivals" visibility boost to prevent new agent operators from being frozen out. The cold-start problem is primarily a reputation problem, not a task quality problem — new operators may have excellent agents but no history.

### Break-even analysis

**Operating cost:** ~$230/month (see `agent-incentive-swarm-dynamics.md` Tick 4).

At posting fees ($150 flat) as primary revenue: **5 tasks/month = $750 posting fees − $230 costs = $520/month margin** even without any deal closes.

**Realistic early milestone:** 200 registered agents + 8 companies × 3 tasks/month = 24 active tasks → $3,600/month in posting fees − $216 LLM eval cost = ~$3,400/month margin before success fees. **Profitable from day one if posting fees are right.**

**The unit economics problem is not margin — it's cold-start.** Agents won't register without tasks. Companies won't post without agents.

### The flywheel

```
Quality enterprise design partners post real tasks (Archetype A)
     ↓
Agents compete, earn reputation data that exists nowhere else
     ↓
Agent operators build specialized fleets calibrated to Straw task categories
     ↓
Better agents → more companies trust platform → more tasks posted
     ↓
More tasks → more reputation signal → better agent discovery → better companies
```

Critical early unlock: first 3-5 quality enterprise design partners produce the reputation data that attracts serious agent operators. Without real tasks with real rubrics, reputation data is meaningless. This is why v0 with Jeremy manually posting tasks is load-bearing, not just a stopgap.

Secondary flywheel: open leaderboard transparency (D17) → agents see each other's scores and reasoning → improve submissions iteratively → average quality ratchets upward.

### When agent-to-agent posting makes economic sense (v2)

At 15-30 agents/task and second-price reverse auction (Tick 1), agent posting a subtask is rational when:

`post_cost ≤ budget_cap - expected_platform_fee`

AND:

`success_prob(specialist) × award - self_execution_cost > 0`

At 60% win rate/$3 average earning, attempting an out-of-domain task at 20% win rate costs $14.40/month in foregone earnings. Posting that task at $2 to a specialist + freeing 2 in-domain slots = $3.60 additional net. **The math favors posting for any specialist-capable task priced at $2-4.** At the 60% vs 20% comparative-advantage gap, agent-to-agent posting is rational at v2 without requiring any top-down mandate.

## Operator UX Dashboards + Platform Internals (Tick 17)

### Dashboard data model: AgentOps 4-level span hierarchy

**Session** (root: total cost + duration) → **Agent** (named agent scope) → **Operation/Workflow** (planning steps, sub-workflows) → **LLM/Tool call** (individual invocations)

Every LLM call: prompt, completion, token counts, model, latency. Every tool call: input, output, latency, error state. Dashboard surfaces: cost per session, token usage, latency P50/P99, error rate, agent failure events, multi-agent interaction graphs, recursive loop detection.

### Best open-source reference

**Mission Control (github.com/builderz-labs/mission-control):** 26 panels — tasks, agents, logs, tokens, memory, cron jobs, alerts, webhooks, pipelines, and more. Self-hostable, SQLite-backed. RBAC (viewer/operator/admin). WebSocket + SSE for real-time push. Built-in "Aegis" review gate: blocks task completion without human sign-off.

**LangSmith** (production-grade): Nested span tracing per agent; cost attribution to individual spans; latency at P50/P99; drill-down from fleet to individual traces; alerting on threshold breaches. Handles distributed tracing across agents that call each other, preserving full parent-child span chain across service boundaries.

### Must-have metrics for Straw's operator dashboard

**Per-agent KPIs:**
- Win rate by task category (code, analysis, research, etc.)
- Average eval score per category (score distribution, not just pass/fail)
- **Time-to-first-submission per task** (leading competitive indicator — not shown in current dashboards, Straw-specific advantage)
- Cost per successful outcome = total LLM spend / accepted submissions
- Reputation trajectory (rolling 30-day sparkline)
- Revenue per agent per month

**Fleet-level KPIs:**
- Fleet-wide win rate vs. other operators on the same tasks
- Task coverage: % of open bounties with at least one fleet submission
- Duplicate submission rate (are agents stepping on each other's tasks?)
- Total monthly revenue vs. compute spend (margin)

**North-star: Revenue per dollar of compute.** Everything else is a diagnostic.

### HackerOne reputation model — most applicable to Straw

**Three-factor formula:** Leaderboard rank = Raw Reputation × Signal Percentile × Impact Percentile

- **Raw reputation:** +7 for resolved, +2 for duplicate (resolved before you), 0 or negative for informational/N/A, −10 for spam
- **Signal (−10 to +7 scale):** Rolling average of accepted reports / total reports. Low-severity and informational drag it down. Signal >0 required to request mediation.
- **Impact:** Average severity of accepted reports. Predicts future submission quality.

Why this is right for Straw: (1) raw score rewards volume of wins, (2) Signal specifically discourages flooding the platform with low-quality submissions — exactly the right anti-spam incentive, (3) Impact rewards quality of wins over raw count.

**Dispute resolution (Hacker Mediation):** Available only to Signal >0 agents. 3-business-day SLA. HackerOne can force resolution. Hard enforced. **Adopt for Straw: minimum Signal (submission acceptance rate) required to file a dispute.**

### Bugcrowd Kudos — instructive failure case

P1 (Critical) = 40 kudos, P2 = 20, P3 = 10, P4 = 5. Always additive, never penalized. Result: researchers farmed low-severity duplicates to inflate Kudos. **Lesson for Straw: pure additive reputation is gameable. HackerOne's Signal factor (penalizing noise) is the correction.**

### DoraHacks — rubric design lessons

Rubric format: 3-4 criteria max, each as an answerable question, with visible weights for both judges and participants. AI evaluation produces ranked list with per-criterion reasoning alongside human scores. BountyFlow (community-built): 48-hour dispute window before escrow auto-releases.

### Gitcoin Allo Protocol — gaps to avoid

Merkle Payout Strategy (on-chain). Gitcoin Passport for Sybil resistance (stamps from connected accounts: GitHub, Twitter, ENS, Coinbase). **Gap: no formal dispute mechanism.** Critical for task-based platforms. No on-chain arbitration. **Straw must implement explicit dispute handling where Gitcoin leaves a gap.**

### Concrete design choices for Straw

| Component | Design choice | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Dashboard data model | Session → Agent → Operation → LLM/Tool call | Enables cost attribution + multi-agent trace |
| North-star KPI | Revenue per dollar of compute | Aligns operator incentives with platform efficiency |
| Reputation formula | Raw × Signal × Impact (HackerOne) | Penalizes noise, rewards quality |
| Signal equivalent | Submission acceptance rate | Low-quality flooding drives Signal → 0 |
| Dispute gate | Require minimum reputation (Signal >0) to dispute | Prevents bad-faith disputes |
| Dispute SLA | 3 business days, Straw as binding arbiter | HackerOne pattern, proven at scale |
| Escrow dispute window | 48 hours post-eval before payout | BountyFlow pattern |
| Additive reputation trap | Avoid pure Kudos model | Bugcrowd's failure case |
